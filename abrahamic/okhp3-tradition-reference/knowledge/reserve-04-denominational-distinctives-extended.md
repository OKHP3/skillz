# Reserve 04 -- Denominational Distinctives Extended

Extended notes on denominational distinctives that exceed what fits in the primary denomination knowledge files. Organized by sub-movement and special category. Agents building denomination-level features, writing comparison content, or answering scope questions should read this file alongside the primary denomination files.

---

## Non-denominational / Independent Evangelical

Non-denominational churches are the fastest-growing segment of American Christianity. They typically share evangelical theology (Bebbington Quadrilateral) without formal denominational affiliation or oversight structure.

**US population context:** included within the ~25% Evangelical Protestant figure (Pew 2023). No separate Pew category.

**Key characteristics:**
- No formal creed beyond Scripture; some use informal statements of faith or a local "What We Believe" page
- Mega-church model is common; services are often contemporary in style (band-led worship, projected lyrics)
- Strong personal relationship with Jesus as a core framing of faith
- Independent governance; no bishop or council above the local congregation
- Wide theological diversity despite shared evangelical identity -- Calvinist, Arminian, and charismatic expressions coexist under the non-denominational umbrella

**Translation preference:** NIV (most common in contemporary evangelical contexts), ESV, KJV. In the free build: `web`, `kjv`, `asv`.

**ARE behavior:** Non-denominational is included within the Evangelical Protestant denomination entry. It does not have a separate denomination page.

## Pentecostal / Charismatic Christianity

Pentecostalism originated in the 1906 Azusa Street Revival in Los Angeles (William J. Seymour). The Charismatic movement (beginning ~1960) brought similar emphases into mainline Protestant and Catholic contexts.

**US population context:** included within the Evangelical Protestant figure (Pew 2023). No separate Pew category at 1%+ threshold in its own right.

**Key characteristics:**
- Emphasis on "gifts of the Spirit" (charismata): speaking in tongues (glossolalia), prophecy, divine healing
- Baptism of the Holy Spirit as a distinct post-conversion experience with speaking in tongues as evidence (classical Pentecostal position)
- Expressive worship; spontaneous prayer; expectation of miraculous signs and wonders
- Global Pentecostalism is the fastest-growing Christian movement worldwide; dominant in sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America
- Catholic Charismatic Renewal: charismatic experience within full Catholic sacramental practice

**Key passages:** Acts 2:1-4 (Pentecost); 1 Corinthians 12-14 (spiritual gifts); Mark 16:17-18 (signs of belief); Romans 8:26 (groaning of the Spirit)

**Translation preference:** NIV, KJV. In the free build: `kjv`, `web`, `bbe`.

**Key US denominations:** Assemblies of God (largest Pentecostal denomination in the US), Church of God in Christ (COGIC), Foursquare Church, United Pentecostal Church International

## Reformed / Calvinist Christianity

A theological tradition within both Evangelical and Mainline Protestantism, emphasizing God's sovereignty in salvation and the comprehensive lordship of Christ over all of life.

**US population context:** distributed across Evangelical and Mainline categories; no separate Pew category.

**Key distinctives:**
- Five Points of Calvinism (TULIP): Total depravity, Unconditional election, Limited atonement, Irresistible grace, Perseverance of the saints
- Westminster Confession of Faith (1646; Presbyterian tradition) or Belgic Confession (1561; Dutch Reformed tradition) as confessional standards
- Covenant theology: the unifying framework of Old and New Covenant as the structure of redemptive history
- Strong tradition of formal theological education and expository preaching
- Cultural mandate: Reformed tradition emphasizes Christian engagement in culture, scholarship, politics, and the arts

**Key denominations:** Presbyterian Church in America (PCA), Reformed Church in America (RCA), Orthodox Presbyterian Church (OPC), Christian Reformed Church (CRC)

**Translation preference:** ESV (dominant in contemporary Reformed circles), KJV (traditional). In the free build: `kjv`, `asv`.

## Lutheran Christianity

The oldest Protestant tradition, founded by Martin Luther's 1517 Reformation in Germany.

**US population context:** included within Mainline Protestant (~16% Pew 2023) for ELCA; some smaller Lutheran bodies lean Evangelical. No separate Pew category.

**Key distinctives:**
- **Sola gratia / sola fide / sola scriptura:** salvation by grace alone through faith alone according to Scripture alone -- the Reformation's three pillars
- **Law and Gospel distinction:** a hermeneutical principle that Scripture must be read as both law (which reveals sin) and gospel (which announces forgiveness); conflating the two is the central Lutheran theological error
- **Real Presence (consubstantiation):** Christ is truly present in, with, and under the bread and wine of communion -- neither Zwinglian symbol nor Catholic transubstantiation
- **Simul justus et peccator:** the believer is simultaneously justified and sinner; sanctification is a process, not a completed state
- **Lutheran Confessions:** the Book of Concord (1580) contains the Augsburg Confession, Luther's Small and Large Catechisms, the Apology of the Augsburg Confession, the Smalcald Articles, the Formula of Concord, and other standards
- **Liturgical worship:** Lutheran services follow a structured liturgy with close ties to Catholic worship forms (though without the Mass in the Catholic sense)

**Key passages:** Romans 1:17 ("the just shall live by faith"); Ephesians 2:8-9 (grace through faith); Galatians 3:10-13 (Christ redeems from the curse of the law); John 6:51-58 (Real Presence)

**Translation preference:** NRSV (ELCA); KJV, ESV (confessional Lutheran bodies). In the free build: `kjv`, `web`, `asv`.

**Key US denominations:** Evangelical Lutheran Church in America (ELCA -- mainline, largest US Lutheran body); Lutheran Church -- Missouri Synod (LCMS -- confessional conservative); Wisconsin Evangelical Lutheran Synod (WELS -- most conservative)

## Baptist Christianity

The largest single Protestant denominational family in the US by total membership, spanning from progressive to deeply conservative.

**US population context:** the majority of Evangelical Protestant figures (~25% Pew 2023) are Baptist or Baptist-adjacent. Southern Baptist Convention alone is the largest Protestant denomination in the US.

**Key distinctives:**
- **Believer's baptism by immersion:** baptism is reserved for those who have made a personal profession of faith; infant baptism is not practiced
- **Congregational polity:** each local church is autonomous; no bishop or denominational authority can bind a local congregation
- **Priesthood of all believers:** every Christian has direct access to God; no priestly mediator between believer and God
- **Separation of church and state:** Baptist tradition (especially in the Roger Williams heritage) championed religious liberty and church-state separation
- **Soul competency / soul liberty:** each individual is responsible before God for their own interpretation of Scripture

**Key passages:** Matthew 28:18-20 (Great Commission -- evangelism); Acts 8:35-38 (believer's baptism); Romans 10:9-10 (confession and faith); 1 Peter 2:9 (royal priesthood)

**Translation preference:** KJV (historically dominant; still preferred in many Southern Baptist contexts), NIV, ESV. In the free build: `kjv`, `web`, `asv`.

**Key US denominations:** Southern Baptist Convention (SBC -- largest Protestant body in the US); American Baptist Churches USA (ABCUSA -- more progressive); National Baptist Convention (largest predominantly Black denomination in the US); Cooperative Baptist Fellowship

## Methodist Christianity

Founded by John Wesley in 18th-century England. Methodism is the theological tradition underlying the United Methodist Church (UMC), the largest single mainline Protestant denomination in the US.

**US population context:** included within Mainline Protestant (~16% Pew 2023).

**Key distinctives:**
- **Wesleyan Quadrilateral:** Scripture, Tradition, Reason, and Experience as four co-equal sources of theological reflection (though Wesley himself gave Scripture primary authority)
- **Prevenient grace:** God's grace precedes human choice and enables the ability to respond to God -- a middle position between Calvinist predestination and Pelagian free will
- **Entire sanctification / Christian perfection:** Wesley's doctrine that a believer can, by grace, be entirely consecrated to love of God and neighbor in this life -- not sinless perfection but freedom from willful sin
- **General Rules:** Wesley's three rules for Methodist societies: do no harm, do good, attend to the ordinances of God
- **Social holiness:** "there is no holiness but social holiness" (Wesley) -- faith is expressed through service, justice, and community action
- **Class meetings and small groups:** Wesley organized Methodists into small accountability groups; this structure pioneered the modern small-group movement

**Key passages:** Matthew 22:37-39 (love God and neighbor); James 2:14-17 (faith without works is dead); 2 Peter 1:3-4 (partakers of the divine nature -- holiness); 1 John 4:7-8 (God is love)

**Translation preference:** NRSV (UMC), KJV (traditional). In the free build: `kjv`, `web`, `asv`.

**Key US denominations:** United Methodist Church (UMC -- split in 2024 over LGBTQ+ inclusion; Global Methodist Church formed as conservative alternative); Free Methodist Church; African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church; African Methodist Episcopal Zion (AME Zion)

## Anglican / Episcopal Christianity

The Episcopal Church (TEC) is the American branch of the worldwide Anglican Communion -- the family of churches in communion with the Archbishop of Canterbury.

**US population context:** included within Mainline Protestant (Pew 2023).

**Key distinctives:**
- **Via media ("middle way"):** Anglicanism occupies a self-described middle position between Catholic and Reformed traditions -- retaining Catholic liturgy and apostolic succession while affirming Protestant soteriology
- **Book of Common Prayer (BCP):** the foundational liturgical text; multiple editions in use (1662 -- traditional; 1979 TEC -- dominant in the US; 2019 ACNA -- conservative Anglican)
- **Apostolic succession through the historic episcopate:** bishops stand in an unbroken line from the apostles (claimed, not universally recognized by other traditions)
- **Three-legged stool:** Richard Hooker's formulation of Scripture, Tradition, and Reason as the three sources of Anglican theological reflection (parallel to the Wesleyan Quadrilateral)
- **Full inclusion (TEC):** The Episcopal Church ordains women and LGBTQ+ clergy at all levels; same-sex marriage is celebrated
- **Anglican Church in North America (ACNA):** conservative Anglican body formed in 2009 in response to TEC's progressive direction; not in communion with Canterbury

**Key passages:** John 1:1-14 (Logos -- high Christology); Matthew 6:9-13 (Lord's Prayer -- central to all Anglican liturgy); 1 Corinthians 11:23-26 (Eucharist institution); Romans 8:38-39 (nothing separates from God's love)

**Translation preference:** NRSV (formal TEC services), KJV (traditional). In the free build: `kjv`, `web`.

## Oriental Orthodox Christianity

Distinct from Eastern (Chalcedonian) Orthodox. The Oriental Orthodox churches rejected the Council of Chalcedon (451 CE) and the Chalcedonian definition of Christ's two natures.

**US population context:** grouped with Orthodox Christian (~1% Pew 2023); no separate Pew category.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Miaphysitism:** Christ has one united nature (divine and human), not two natures as in Chalcedonian Orthodoxy; the "one nature" is the point of theological difference
- The Oriental Orthodox do not consider themselves monophysite (one nature only) -- the term miaphysite is preferred as it preserves the unity of natures without collapsing them
- **Major churches:** Armenian Apostolic Church, Coptic Orthodox Church (Egypt), Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church, Eritrean Orthodox Tewahedo Church, Syriac Orthodox Church, Malankara Orthodox Syrian Church (India)
- **Liturgical languages:** Arabic (Coptic liturgy), Ge'ez (Ethiopian), Classical Syriac, Armenian
- **Ancient foundations:** the Coptic and Ethiopian churches trace their origins to the apostolic era (Philip and the Ethiopian eunuch, Acts 8:26-39; St. Mark's mission to Egypt)

**ARE behavior:** grouped within the Orthodox Christian denomination entry. The SKILL.md and Christianity-orthodox.md cover Eastern Orthodox as the primary reference; this extended note distinguishes Oriental Orthodox for agents who need the precision.

**API coverage:** same as Eastern Orthodox -- `web`, `kjv` via bible-api.com. Full Oriental Orthodox canon varies; Ethiopian Orthodox has the broadest canon in Christendom (~81 books, including 1 Enoch).

## Community of Christ (formerly RLDS)

The second-largest Restorationist body, historically known as the Reorganized Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints (RLDS).

**US population context:** too small to register separately in Pew data; grouped within LDS / Restorationist (~2%).

**Key distinctions from LDS:**
- Accepts Joseph Smith's founding narrative but has diverged significantly in theology, governance, and practice since the 19th-century succession crisis
- Ordains women to all levels of ministry (since 1984)
- Accepts LGBTQ+ members and marriage (ongoing)
- Uses the NRSV and other modern translations; not committed to KJV
- Temple in Independence, Missouri (2001) -- built as a place of peace and reconciliation; open to all; contrasts with LDS temples (restricted access)
- Scripture: uses the Book of Mormon (though in a slightly different text) and Doctrine and Covenants; does not include the Pearl of Great Price as canonical

**ARE behavior:** grouped within the LDS / Restorationist denomination entry. ARE does not have a separate Community of Christ denomination page.

## Messianic Judaism

Jewish believers who accept Jesus (Yeshua) as the Messiah while maintaining Jewish identity and practice.

**Scope note:** Messianic Judaism is a hybrid category -- Jewish ethnic/cultural identity combined with Christian theological affirmation. ARE treats it as context-only and does not display it as a separate denomination or tradition. It does not meet the 1% US threshold as a distinct tradition.

**ARE behavior:** not displayed as a denomination; not included in cross-tradition comparisons as a separate category. When Messianic Judaism is mentioned in user queries, route to Judaism or Christianity (Evangelical Protestant) depending on context. Do not blend the two tradition entries.

## Seventh-day Adventist

**US population context:** below 1% US threshold (Pew 2023); not in ARE scope as a separate category.

**Scope status:** OUT OF SCOPE (below 1% US population threshold per Pew 2023). Do not include as a denomination in ARE.

**Context note for agents:** Seventh-day Adventist (SDA) is a Restorationist-adjacent tradition with distinctive beliefs including Saturday (seventh-day) Sabbath observance, health reform, investigative judgment doctrine, and the prophetic ministry of Ellen G. White. Its US population is insufficient for the 1% threshold. If a user asks about SDA, confirm scope limits and point to the closest in-scope entry (Evangelical Protestant for shared Protestant theology).

## Jehovah's Witnesses

**US population context:** below 1% US threshold (Pew 2023); not in ARE scope.

**Scope status:** OUT OF SCOPE (below 1% US population threshold per Pew 2023). Do not include in ARE.

**Context note for agents:** Jehovah's Witnesses use the New World Translation (NWT), a translation produced by the Watch Tower Society and not available via any free API. They reject the Trinity, celebrate no holidays, and do not accept blood transfusions. Their theological distinctives place them outside the doctrinal mainstream of the five in-scope Christian denominations. Confirm scope limits if asked.

## Black Protestant Christianity

The National Baptist Convention, the African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church, the Church of God in Christ (COGIC), and other historically Black denominations together constitute one of the largest and most distinct expressions of American Christianity.

**US population context:** the Pew Research Center reports Black Protestants as a significant sub-category within the total Evangelical Protestant figure; no independent 1%+ separate category. Included within Evangelical Protestant in ARE.

**Key characteristics:**
- The Black Church emerged in the context of American slavery and its aftermath; worship, theology, and community life are inseparable from that history
- Freedom, justice, and liberation theology are central -- the Exodus narrative and the prophetic tradition (Amos, Isaiah) are foundational texts
- Call-and-response preaching; expressive worship; music (spirituals, gospel) as theological expression
- Strong community institution: the church as civic center, mutual aid society, and political organizing base
- Theological diversity: from conservative evangelical (COGIC) to liberation-theology-influenced (AME, some Baptist bodies)

**Key passages:** Amos 5:24 ("Let justice roll like a river"); Luke 4:18-19 (Jesus's mission: "to proclaim freedom for the prisoners"); Exodus 3:7-10 (God hears the cry of the oppressed); Romans 8:31 ("If God is for us, who can be against us?")

**Translation preference:** KJV (historic; deeply embedded in Black church culture and liturgy), NIV, NRSV. In the free build: `kjv`, `web`, `asv`.

**Key denominations:** National Baptist Convention USA, Inc. (largest historically Black denomination); National Baptist Convention of America; Progressive National Baptist Convention; African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church; African Methodist Episcopal Zion (AME Zion); Church of God in Christ (COGIC -- Pentecostal)

**ARE behavior:** included within the Evangelical Protestant denomination entry. ARE does not have a separate Black Protestant denomination page. When discussing liberation theology or justice-oriented readings, these communities are the primary referent within the Evangelical Protestant category.
